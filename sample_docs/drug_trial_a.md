# Randomized trial of drug alphaxolin in moderate hypertension

- Trial ID: SAMPLE-HTX-001
- Status: Example document for SLIDERS. Not a real study.

## Abstract

**Background.** Alphaxolin is an investigational selective vasodilator proposed for the management of moderate essential hypertension in adults who remain above target on monotherapy.

**Methods.** This was a double-blind, placebo-controlled, parallel-group randomized trial conducted at 12 outpatient cardiology centers. Adults aged 40-70 years with sustained seated systolic blood pressure between 140 and 160 mmHg on a single background antihypertensive agent were randomly assigned in a 1:1 ratio to receive oral alphaxolin 20 mg once daily or matching placebo for 12 weeks. The primary endpoint was change from baseline in mean 24-hour ambulatory systolic blood pressure at week 12.

**Results.** A total of 420 participants were randomized (210 per arm). Mean age was 58.4 years and 46% were female. At week 12, the adjusted mean change in 24-hour systolic blood pressure was −8.1 mmHg (95% CI −9.6 to −6.6) in the alphaxolin group and −2.3 mmHg (95% CI −3.8 to −0.8) in the placebo group, for a between-group difference of −5.8 mmHg (P < 0.001). Treatment-emergent adverse events occurred in 18% of alphaxolin and 15% of placebo participants. Three serious adverse events occurred in the alphaxolin group and two in the placebo group; none were judged related to study drug.

**Conclusions.** Alphaxolin added to background antihypertensive therapy produced a clinically meaningful reduction in 24-hour ambulatory blood pressure over 12 weeks with an acceptable safety profile.

## Methods

### Design
Multicenter, double-blind, placebo-controlled, parallel-group randomized trial. Participants, investigators, and outcome adjudicators were masked to treatment assignment.

### Participants
Eligible participants were adults 40-70 years of age with seated systolic BP 140-160 mmHg on a stable dose of a single first-line antihypertensive (ACE inhibitor, ARB, calcium channel blocker, or thiazide) for at least eight weeks prior to enrollment. Key exclusions were secondary hypertension, prior cardiovascular event within six months, eGFR < 45 mL/min/1.73m², and pregnancy.

### Intervention
Alphaxolin 20 mg orally once daily, or matching placebo, for 12 weeks, added to the participant's existing antihypertensive regimen.

### Outcomes
The primary outcome was the change from baseline in mean 24-hour ambulatory systolic blood pressure at week 12. Secondary outcomes included change in office systolic blood pressure, change in diastolic blood pressure, and safety assessed by treatment-emergent adverse events.

## Results

### Participants
Between March 2024 and August 2024, 512 participants were screened and 420 were randomized (210 alphaxolin, 210 placebo). Baseline characteristics were balanced. Of randomized participants, 198 in the alphaxolin group and 202 in the placebo group completed week 12.

### Primary outcome
Adjusted mean change in 24-hour ambulatory systolic BP at week 12 was −8.1 mmHg in the alphaxolin group and −2.3 mmHg in the placebo group; between-group difference −5.8 mmHg (95% CI −7.5 to −4.1; P < 0.001).

### Secondary outcomes
Office systolic BP decreased by −9.4 mmHg (alphaxolin) vs −3.1 mmHg (placebo). Office diastolic BP decreased by −5.2 mmHg vs −1.6 mmHg.

### Adverse events
Treatment-emergent adverse events were reported in 37/210 (18%) alphaxolin and 32/210 (15%) placebo participants. The most common events in the alphaxolin group were headache (6%), dizziness (4%), and peripheral edema (3%).

## Discussion

In adults with moderate hypertension uncontrolled on monotherapy, adding alphaxolin reduced 24-hour ambulatory systolic blood pressure by approximately 6 mmHg more than placebo over 12 weeks. The effect was consistent across prespecified subgroups including age, sex, baseline background therapy class, and presence of diabetes.

The trial has several limitations. The 12-week duration is too short to assess cardiovascular outcomes. Background therapy was restricted to monotherapy, so the findings may not generalize to patients on multiple antihypertensives. The study population was predominantly from high-income countries.

## Conclusion

Alphaxolin 20 mg daily added to background antihypertensive therapy produced a clinically meaningful reduction in 24-hour ambulatory blood pressure with an acceptable short-term safety profile. Longer trials powered on cardiovascular outcomes are warranted before routine use can be recommended.
